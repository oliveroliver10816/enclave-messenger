Everything a team needs. Nothing a stranger can read.

Enclave keeps the ergonomics of the group chat you already know — channels, threads, calls, files — and strips out every place your conversation could leak.

Sealed by default

Every message, call and file is end-to-end encrypted with X3DH key agreement and a Double Ratchet. The server only ever holds ciphertext it can't open.

You own the server

Ships as one self-contained binary or a single Docker command. Runs on a $5 VPS, a Pi, or the box in your closet. No cloud, no vendor account, no bill from us.

Invite-only enclave

No public directory, no open sign-up, no phone number. Membership is a one-time cryptographic invite token — and you can revoke anyone in a click.

Zero-knowledge storage

Messages, media and backups sit encrypted at rest. A stolen disk or a legal demand for your server yields nothing but noise.

Built for distance

Offline queue, delta sync and a low-bandwidth mode keep a team across six timezones and bad hotel Wi-Fi perfectly in step.

Verify your people

Compare a short safety number or scan a QR out-of-band to lock out any man-in-the-middle. If someone's key changes, everyone hears about it loudly.

Disappearing by design

Per-channel timers, burn-on-read, and a global “vanish” that shreds history on every linked device at once.

Encrypted voice & video

Small-group E2EE calls and screen share over SRTP, relayed through a TURN server you host yourself — media never touches anyone else's hardware.

Metadata starvation

Padded packets and sealed-sender routing hide who is talking to whom, and when — the pattern is often as sensitive as the words.

// the seal

Three steps, and the server goes blind.

There's no “trust us” in the loop. Keys are made and kept on your devices; the server is a dumb relay for envelopes it can't open.

01

Keys are born on-device

Each device generates its own long-term and one-time keys locally. Private keys never leave the hardware — not in a backup, not to the server, not to us.

02

Every message re-ratchets

A Double Ratchet rolls a fresh key forward for each message, so a key captured today can't unlock yesterday's — or tomorrow's — conversation.

03

The server just forwards noise

What lands on your box is a sealed envelope: padded, sender-hidden ciphertext. Compromise the server and you get a pile of random bytes.

One command. Your enclave is live.

If you can paste a line into a terminal, you can run it. Point a domain at it, open one port, invite your people — done.

  • Runs anywhere. A $5/mo VPS, a home server, or a Raspberry Pi 4 handles a team of a dozen without breaking a sweat.
  • No accounts, anywhere. Nothing to register with us. There is no “us” in the data path.
  • Encrypted backups you hold. Snapshot to your own S3-compatible bucket — encrypted before it ever leaves the box.
  • Reproducible builds. Verify the binary you run matches the source you read. No blind trust in a download.

The questions a careful team asks first.

Can the server really not read our messages?
Correct. Encryption and decryption happen only on member devices using keys that never leave them. The server's job is to hold and forward sealed envelopes. Even with full root on the box, an attacker sees padded ciphertext and nothing else — no plaintext, and with sealed-sender, not even a clean map of who messaged whom.
What happens if someone loses their laptop or phone?
Each device carries its own key, so you remote-wipe and de-link just that device — the rest of the team keeps working, no group-wide key rotation, no downtime. A duress passphrase and instant local panic-wipe are there for the worse-case moments too.
How big a team is this actually for?
Enclave is deliberately built for small, high-trust groups — think 3 to ~50 people who all know each other. It's not trying to be a 10,000-seat company Slack. That constraint is what lets it stay invite-only, verifiable, and simple to self-host.
Will it work from countries with heavy filtering?
Traffic looks like ordinary TLS to a standard web port, with an optional pluggable-transport mode to blend in further. Because you choose where the server lives and what domain fronts it, you're not tied to any one blockable endpoint.
What does it cost?
The software is free and open source under MIT. Your only real cost is the little server you run it on — a few dollars a month, or nothing if you already have a machine at home.
